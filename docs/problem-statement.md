# RLC Café POS — What This System Solves

## 1. Queue Bottleneck with Limited Volunteers

You have 2-3 people running a café serving an entire church congregation across two tight 75-minute windows. Previously, one person takes orders, handles payment, AND makes drinks. The physical queue blocks everything — if 10 people line up at once, the first drink doesn't start until the 10th order is taken.

Self-ordering eliminates the queue entirely. All 10 people order simultaneously from their phones. The cashier only verifies payments. The barista just makes drinks.

**Additionally:** Customers can start ordering during the sermon — before the café window even opens. By the time service ends and they walk to the counter, their order is already in the queue. No rush, no crowd at the counter during peak transition.

## 2. Inconsistent Inventory Tracking

The stock check spreadsheet shows weekly manual counts with messy data — fractions like "1/8", missing entries, inconsistent date formats across 100+ columns. Nobody knows mid-service if they're about to run out of oat milk. The restock person checks "mid-week, inconsistent." This means some Sundays you run out of ingredients with no warning.

The system gives real-time estimated stock (recipe-based deduction), low-stock alerts, and AI-powered photo counting (planogram) to make the weekly check take 30 seconds instead of 10 minutes.

## 3. Payment Reconciliation Pain

Previously, the cashier manually keys in every order AND manually matches DuitNow payments. End-of-day reconciliation is basically "count the money and hope it matches." With newcomers and staff getting free/discounted drinks, offsets are tracked mentally.

The system automatically tracks total revenue, discount offsets (newcomer, staff, pastor, celebration), and net expected — giving you a clean end-of-day number to reconcile against the Maybank account.

## 4. Volunteer Onboarding & Accountability

New volunteers previously need to learn the POS system, memorize menu prices, figure out which items are available today, and remember special pricing rules. High friction means fewer people volunteer.

With this system:
- **Open/close checklists** guide volunteers step-by-step: turn on machine, fill ice, test shot, etc. No training needed — just follow the list.
- Checklist is **blocking** — can't open café until everything is done. Ensures nothing is forgotten.
- Completion is **logged** (who, what time) — accountability without micromanagement.
- Menu items pre-configured with correct prices, discount buttons built in, order board manages itself.

## 5. Food Availability Chaos

Food is "subject to availability" from other ministries — different items each week, unpredictable quantities. Previously this is word-of-mouth: "we have 5 nasi lemak today." Customers don't know what's available until they reach the counter.

Now food shows up on the customer menu with live quantity counts. When it's gone, it disappears. No one walks up expecting something that's sold out.

## 6. Planogram — AI-Powered Stock Counting

Manual stock counts are tedious, error-prone, and often skipped. The spreadsheet has gaps where nobody recorded anything. This creates a "garbage in, garbage out" problem for restock planning.

The planogram feature solves this by:
- **Reducing effort**: Snap 2 photos (fridge + storeroom) → AI counts everything in seconds
- **Increasing accuracy**: AI compares against a reference photo of the ideal arrangement, identifying specific brands and quantities
- **Enforcing consistency**: Built into the open/close checklist, so it happens every service without exception
- **Building history**: 4 weeks of photos for trend analysis — "we always run low on oat milk by the 3rd Sunday"

The reference photo approach also standardizes how volunteers arrange stock — making both the AI and human scanning faster.

## 7. Full Ownership & Flexibility to Expand

Unlike Loyverse (third-party, locked features, subscription tiers), this system is fully owned:
- **No vendor lock-in**: all code, all data, all infrastructure under your control
- **Zero subscription cost**: serverless architecture means you pay only for usage (~$1-2/month)
- **Unlimited customization**: church-specific features (celebration mode, newcomer discounts, pastor pricing) that no generic POS would ever build
- **Can expand scope freely**: add new features as needs emerge — display screens, kitchen printer integration, volunteer scheduling, multi-site support — without waiting for a vendor roadmap

## 8. Data Capture for Future Intelligence

Every order, every stock count, every service day generates data. This isn't just operational — it's strategic:

- **Order preferences**: Know which drinks are popular, which food items sell out fastest, what newcomers typically order
- **Traffic patterns**: Understand volume differences between normal Sundays, Praise Day, Easter, Christmas Week, etc. — plan stock accordingly
- **Seasonal trends**: "Iced drinks spike in March-October, hot drinks in November-February"
- **Volunteer load**: How many orders per service? Is 2 volunteers enough or do special days need 3-4?
- **Restock prediction**: Based on consumption rate, predict when to reorder before running out — not after
- **Ministry coordination**: Food contribution data helps coordinate with other ministries ("we consistently sell out of kueh by 10:45 — can we get 20 instead of 10?")

This data doesn't need to be acted on immediately. But capturing it now means when the café grows or leadership asks "how are we doing?", the answers are already there.

## Summary

This system is a **volunteer force multiplier** — letting 2 people do what normally takes 4-5 by removing human bottlenecks (order-taking, payment verification, inventory counting, reconciliation) and replacing them with self-service, automation, and AI assistance. It turns a stressful Sunday rush into a calm, guided workflow.
